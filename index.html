How to Safely Put Out Fires

A plain-language guide to fire types, what works, what makes things worse, and when your only job is to get out and call for help.

First rule: know when NOT to fight a fire

Most injuries happen when someone tries to be a hero. Fire spreads in seconds. Only attempt to put out a fire if all of these are true:

  • The fire is small — about the size of a wastepaper basket or smaller.
  • You have the right tool (correct extinguisher or safe method).
  • You are not trapped and have a clear exit behind you.
  • The room is not full of smoke — smoke kills faster than flames.
  • You are not alone in a large building — others may need help evacuating.
Leave immediately if: the fire grows, you can't breathe, the extinguisher runs empty, you don't know what's burning, or you're unsure. Close doors behind you to slow spread. Call 911 from outside.

Five types of fire — use the wrong method and it explodes

Fires are grouped by what is burning. Water saves one type and detonates another. Match the method to the class.

CLASS A

Ordinary solids

Wood, paper, cloth, cardboard, most plastics, furniture.

Water, ABC dry chemical, foam. Cool and soak until no heat remains.
Use water on any other class without knowing it's safe.
CLASS B

Flammable liquids & gases

Gasoline, paint, oil (not cooking grease — see Class K), propane, solvents.

BC or ABC dry chemical, CO₂, or foam. Shut off gas if safe.
Water — it spreads burning liquid and can cause a violent flare-up.
CLASS C

Electrical equipment

Anything plugged in or wired while energized: appliances, panels, chargers.

Turn off power at the breaker if safe, then treat as Class A. Use CO₂ or ABC while energized.
Water on live equipment — risk of shock and worse fire.
CLASS D

Combustible metals

Magnesium, titanium, sodium, potassium — labs and some industrial sites.

Only a Class D dry powder extinguisher rated for that metal.
Water, foam, or ABC — can cause violent reactions or hydrogen explosions.
CLASS K

Kitchen cooking oils & fats

Deep fryers, pans of oil, animal fat — common at home and in restaurants.

Smother with a metal lid, Class K wet chemical, or baking soda on tiny fires.
Water — it instantly turns to steam and throws burning oil everywhere.

Using a fire extinguisher — the PASS method

Stand 6–8 feet (2 m) away with your exit at your back. Aim low at the base of the flame, not the top.

PPull the pin
AAim at the base of the fire
SSqueeze the handle
SSweep side to side slowly
Check the label before you use it. Home kitchens benefit from an ABC extinguisher and knowing how to smother grease fires. A typical extinguisher only gives you about 10 seconds of spray.

Which extinguisher to buy

  • Home / office: ABC dry chemical (5 lb or larger), mounted near exits — not buried in a closet.
  • Kitchen: ABC plus a metal lid nearby; consider a small Class K if you deep-fry often.
  • Garage / workshop: ABC; keep flammable liquids in closed metal cabinets away from heat.
  • Check yearly: gauge in the green zone, pin intact, no damage. Replace or recharge after any use.

Common situations — step by step

🔥 Grease fire on the stove

  1. Turn off the burner if you can reach it safely — no moving the pan.
  2. Slide a metal lid over the pan to cut off oxygen. Leave it on until completely cool (30+ minutes).
  3. If no lid: use a Class K or ABC extinguisher aimed at the base — not a blast from above.
  4. Tiny flare-up only: handful of baking soda (not baking powder, not flour).
Never use water, wet towel, or fan — they spread burning grease.

🔥 Oven or microwave fire

  1. Keep the door closed — oxygen feeds fire.
  2. Turn off the appliance and unplug if reachable without opening the door.
  3. If flames escape the appliance or don't die quickly: evacuate and call 911.

🔥 Trash can or small paper fire

  1. Pull the bin away from walls and curtains if safe.
  2. Use water, blanket, or ABC extinguisher until cold.
  3. Watch for re-ignition — embers can smolder inside paper.

🔥 Electrical outlet or appliance

  1. Shut off power at the circuit breaker — not just the switch.
  2. Use ABC or CO₂ if fire continues; never water while energized.
  3. Have an electrician inspect before turning the circuit back on.

🔥 Gasoline, paint, or garage fluid fire

  1. Shut off the fuel source if you can without crossing the fire.
  2. Use ABC dry chemical or foam — not water.
  3. Evacuate if the pool of liquid is larger than a dinner plate.

🔥 Clothes on fire (on a person)

  1. Stop, Drop, and Roll — don't run (fanning adds oxygen).
  2. Smother with a heavy coat or blanket; cool burns with water after flames are out.
  3. Call 911 — even small burns need medical attention.

🔥 Candle, cigarette, or sofa cushion

  1. Remove oxygen: snuff candle, smother cushion with heavy blanket.
  2. Douse with water once flames are out if material is Class A (fabric, wood).
  3. If cushion or sofa is fully involved: leave — foam furniture burns fast and releases toxic smoke.

🔥 Wildfire / brush near your home

  1. Follow evacuation orders immediately — garden hoses cannot stop a wildfire.
  2. Close all windows and doors if you have time while leaving.
  3. Do not stay to "spray the roof" unless officials say it's safe and you have a clear escape route.

Special cases & myths

Smoke is the real killer

Stay low under smoke. Before opening a closed door, feel it with the back of your hand. If it's hot, use another exit. Smoke one breath can disorient you — that's when people get trapped.

Don't use flour on grease fires

Flour and powdered sugar are combustible dust. Baking soda works on tiny fires because it releases CO₂. A fire blanket or lid is more reliable.

CO₂ extinguishers

Good for electronics and liquids. No cleanup residue, but the discharge is extremely cold and can displace oxygen in small rooms — ventilate and leave if the room feels stuffy.

Halon / clean agents

Found in server rooms and aircraft. Effective on sensitive equipment. Follow facility training — not typical for homes.

After any fire: Even if you think it's out, check for hidden heat with the back of your hand near walls and ceilings. Re-ignite is common. When in doubt, call the fire department — they'd rather inspect a small incident than fight a house fire.

Prevention beats every extinguisher

  • Install smoke alarms on every level; test monthly; replace batteries or units per manufacturer (typically every 10 years).
  • Never leave cooking unattended — most home fires start in the kitchen.
  • Keep space heaters 3 feet from anything flammable; plug directly into wall outlets.
  • Store gasoline and solvents outside living areas in approved containers.
  • Don't overload power strips; replace frayed cords.
  • Plan two exits from every room and a meeting spot outside. Practice with your household twice a year.
  • Consider a fire blanket in the kitchen — simple, no expiry, works on small pan fires and clothing.
